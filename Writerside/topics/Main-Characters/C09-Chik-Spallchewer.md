Backstory
Chik Spallchewer doesn't remember his parents. He doesn't remember the station he was born on, or the circumstances that left him in Grandmother Niska's creche. What he remembers is the dark—the endless, star-scattered dark of the Diaspora, and the warm, crowded safety of the creche within it.
Grandmother Niska was ancient even by Ysoki standards, her fur gone grey-white, her eyes still sharp as scanner arrays. She ran the creche like a small kingdom—no, like a ship. Every Ysoki kit had a job, a role, a purpose. Chik's was small hands in tight spaces: retrieving salvage from ducts too narrow for adults, slipping through maintenance corridors to "acquire" supplies. He learned that being small wasn't a weakness if you were smart enough.
The other kits called him "Spallchewer" after he was caught gnawing through a chunk of asteroid debris to reach a sealed compartment. (The compartment held emergency rations. Grandmother Niska said it was "initiative." The station's supply officer called it theft. Grandmother Niska paid him in extra portions and a warning to be sneakier.)
At night, Niska told stories. Stories of Grandmother Rat—the endless hunger, the devourer of worlds, the one who gnaws at the roots of reality. And stories of those who stood against her: Quick-Paw Who Stole Fire from the heart of a star to warm the first burrows. Gnaw-Tooth Who Chewed Through Chains to free the enslaved. The Rat-That-Would-Not-Be-Eaten, who tricked Grandmother Rat into swallowing a sun and fled while she burned. Chik loved those stories. He fell asleep imagining himself as one of them—small, clever, brave.
At ten, he met Lowda Medina—a male Sarcesian, grey-skinned and towering, placed in the creche by parents who loved him too much to drag him through their dangerous salvage work. He didn't fit. Neither did Chik. They became friends in the way of outsiders: quietly, fiercely, without needing to explain. Lowda taught him that the universe was bigger than the Diaspora, stranger than salvage, full of possibilities he couldn't imagine. He taught Lowda to pick locks and which ration packs were worth trading for. They talked about leaving, together, someday—maybe even finding out if the old stories were true.
At sixteen, he made the mistake. A group of salvagers needed a guide through a debris field. Paid well. Chik, hungry for something more than scavenger's wages, took the job. He didn't realize they were pirates until it was too late—until they hit a station he'd sworn was abandoned. Three people died because of his information. Echo, the information broker who'd brokered the job, pulled strings to get him out before the survivors could ask too many questions. Chik has carried that weight ever since.
He left the creche at eighteen, officially "aged out," though Grandmother Niska gave him a look that said she'd always know where he was. Lowda had already gone—his own path, his own adventures, though his parents' work meant he was harder to track. They promised to stay in touch, and they have, sporadically, through crackling comms and the occasional data-chip left at dead drops. Lowda's voice is always warm. He never asks about the three people. Chik loves him for that.
The years since have been survival: odd jobs, legitimate and less-so, always moving, always watching. He learned to shoot because the universe is full of things that want to eat small rats. He learned to hack because information is currency. He learned to be invisible because being seen gets you killed.
And through it all, he carried the stories. Quick-Paw. Gnaw-Tooth. The Rat-That-Would-Not-Be-Eaten. They were just stories—until the Scream.
He was on a transport, minding his own business, when the wave hit. He remembers light, sound, pain—and then knowing, somehow, that something had changed. The energy inside him now, the warmth, the whisper of power—it's not his. But it's in him, and when he picks up his rifle, he feels... more. Aiming feels like prayer. Throwing his needle feels like telling a story.
And the stories don't feel like stories anymore. They feel like instructions.
He doesn't know what it means. But he's started reading deeper—mythology texts, historical accounts, anything that might tell him if the heroes were real. If their power could be reborn. If a rat from a creche in the dark could walk the same path.
And then the pirates grabbed him. Stupid—complacent on that transport, not watching the exits like Niska taught. Now he's in a cell, the Scream's residue thrumming in his bones, his rifle somewhere in this ship, his needle hidden against his skin, and a cellmate somewhere in the shadows.
Grandmother Niska used to end her stories the same way: "The heroes didn't know they were heroes until the moment they had to be. Before that, they were just rats—hungry, scared, small. But when the time came, they chewed through what held them back."
Time to see if he's a hero. Or just a rat in a cage.
